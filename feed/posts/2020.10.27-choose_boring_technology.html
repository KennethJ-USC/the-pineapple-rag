Tuesday Commentary: Choose Boring Technology

Choose Boring Technology
Dan McKinley

Link to original article: Choose Boring Technology

Editor's note: I'll be playing around with different sorts of articles and commentary over the next few weeks. Your feedback and engagement is more than appreciated. I'm starting with Choose Boring Technology because it addresses an important (but underappreciated) topic in an entertaining, brief, and accessible format. It's certainly a favorite of mine.

Technology is complicated, and maintaining technology over any noteworthy amount of time is even harder. Assuming that digital systems naturally mature and stabilize over time is a dangerous assumption. Digital systems consist of many things, most importantly technologies, tools, people, and processes. This article deals primarily with the first two.

Importantly, decisions over which technologies to invest in - and which to pass up on - are critical to the long-term stability of that system. Indeed, Dan's primary audience are the individuals and teams faced with these decisions.

Embrace Boredom. "Boring" is not bad. Boring technology is often the best tool for the job, precisely because it is boring. Boring technologies tend to be…

...Stable (PostgreSQL, Windows, inSite),
...Interoperable/Standardized (email, Java),
...Mature (.NET, Red Hat, Wikis),
...Trustworthy (email, IRC).

Sometimes, of course, you'll want to choose new technologies. Innovative, exciting solutions can be the best choice. However, that decisions ought to be the result of a calculation that considers the costs and risks associated with technology that hasn't proven itself yet to be boring. Boring technology doesn't happen by accident.

Questions:

  • Do you think this is relevant to Boeing ITDA? How so?
  • What are some boring technologies you appreciate?
  • Are there examples of areas/problem spaces/technologies where we ought to explore newer options at the risk of added complexity? (e.g. collaborative tools, like Mattermost)

Next week we'll take a look at the opposite sides of the problem: when investment in new technologies makes the most sense.
